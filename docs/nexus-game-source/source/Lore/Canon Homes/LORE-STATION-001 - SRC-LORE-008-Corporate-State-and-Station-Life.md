# Corporate, State, and Station Life

## Station life premise

Most people in Nexus survive inside systems someone else owns, regulates, maintains, powers, insures, or can shut off. Station life should be practical, dense, monitored, legalistic, and dependent on maintenance.

A station is not just a location. It is food, air, identity, work, medicine, credentials, pressure doors, cameras, waste heat, labor schedules, seals, dispute procedures, and emergency authority.

## Corporate citizenship

Corporate citizenship is not only greed or villainy. It can be a survival bargain:

- work/service for housing;
- obedience for medical care;
- contract for legal identity;
- implant plan for employability;
- debt for oxygen, repair, and protection;
- surveillance for stability.

This makes corporate power believable and morally uncomfortable.

## State and treaty presence

Treaty or system authorities remain important because habitats need recognized identity, traffic control, legal process, custody chains, evidence standards, emergency response, and conflict arbitration.

Their weakness is often speed and jurisdiction, not total absence.

## Everyday pressures

Station life should produce playable tensions:

- rationing and maintenance windows;
- access credentials and revoked permissions;
- corporate clinics and debt;
- pressure-suit norms and seal discipline;
- identity scans and legal records;
- repair crew politics;
- splicer/cyborg/upload accommodation disputes;
- implant requirements for certain jobs;
- local rumor networks;
- emergency powers in sealed environments;
- surveillance justified by survival risk.


## Standard Fit as infrastructure culture

Standard Fit is not only a character/equipment rule. It is a station-life assumption. Habitats, suits, crash couches, restraints, medkits, uniforms, doors, workstations, clinics, emergency shelters, and custody systems often assume a narrow range of bodies unless the location has deliberately adapted.

This makes near-baseline and Standard Fit people easier to process, rescue, restrain, hire, house, insure, and evacuate. It also makes nonstandard bodies expensive, politically visible, or easy to exclude under the language of safety and procedure.

## Vatborn, Designer Human, and Splicer social pressure

Vatborn / Low Caste station pressure may include:

- being treated as assigned-function labor rather than independent citizen;
- service assumptions and back-of-house access;
- testimony/status credibility problems;
- being ignored by elites;
- difficulty asserting authority over born-family citizens, patrons, officers, or designer-line aristocrats;
- access to low-status worker networks and spaces where elites stop performing politeness.

Designer Human station pressure may include:

- family-line prestige and recognizable signature traits;
- inherited presentation as social credential;
- expectations to display lineage aesthetics;
- pressure to conceal, advertise, or authenticate curated traits.

Splicer accommodation disputes may include:

- habitat access;
- suit compatibility;
- seating, restraints, crash couches, and airlocks;
- medical care and legal classification;
- employment category and hazard assignments;
- prejudice mixed with practical infrastructure friction.

## Mission hooks

Station life can generate missions without needing galactic-scale stakes:

- escort a whistleblower out of a corporate habitat;
- investigate a custody-chain irregularity;
- steal or validate a credential;
- recover a lost personhood record;
- repair a failing habitat system while factions argue over access;
- protect a clinic from forced de-augmentation or coercive implantation;
- reveal whether a "routine" corporate order is Choir-shaped.

## Source caution

This document seeds station life from established principles. It does not yet contain a complete station-life bible. Further Seed/Draft work should expand food, housing, religion, family law, entertainment, markets, maintenance culture, and local slang.
